# CHAPTER 14: THE IRON LUNG

The Iron Lung didn’t breathe; it processed. 

Standing at the base of the spire, the structure loomed over the Sinks like a monolith of unrefined malice. It was the Empire’s central processing hub—a brutalist hive of chrome and obsidian where the Aether-Cores of the fallen were scrubbed of their memories and repurposed into fuel. It didn't smell like a factory; it smelled like the cloying sweetness of fermented memories being burned off, a psychic exhaust that clung to the back of my throat.

"Throttling at sixty percent," I whispered, my fingers dancing over the diagnostic interface projected on my wrist. The amber glow was dim, a deliberate choice to avoid detection. "The aether-fog in this tier is high. It’s like breathing static, Kaelen. My core is struggling to filter the noise."

"Focus on the rhythm of the machine, Caelia. Not the air," he replied. 

I hated how well he knew my hardware. He wasn’t just a commander anymore; he was a technician who had learned the exact frequency of my burnout. Since the alleyway—since he’d leveled a city block's worth of threats just because one had touched my arm—the air between us had changed. It was denser. Charged. 

Behind us, Silas moved with the silent grace of a unit that had been factory-calibrated for stealth. Kaelen’s second-in-command was the only one Kaelen trusted to watch our extraction point. 

"Entry point clear," Silas whispered over the comms. "The guards are on a five-minute cycle. Move now, or you’re just expensive scrap."

"Moving," Kaelen said. 

I followed him into the vent, the metal cold against my palms. My muscles felt over-clocked, a dull ache radiating from the base of my skull where the interface sat. This was what I was built for. This was the "Prodigy" at work. But as we crawled through the dark, the Iron Lung’s artificial heart thrummed through the floor, a low-frequency pulse that reminded me I was nothing but a biological component in a much larger, much deadlier machine.

# CHAPTER 15: LATENCY

Internal security in the Iron Lung wasn't handled by men. It was handled by the *Sentinels*—deactivated God-Machines reduced to autonomous husks that patrolled the hallways with thermal-sensitive optics.

"Wait," I hissed, grabbing the back of Kaelen’s tactical vest. 

He froze instantly. We were pressed into a recessed alcove, the obsidian wall sapping the heat from my shoulder. 

A Sentinel drifted past, its violet eye sweeping the floor. The sound of its anti-grav plates was a sickening, metallic whine. If it saw us, it wouldn't just sound an alarm; it would vent the hallway with liquid aether, flash-freezing everything into glass.

"It's on a predictive algorithm," I whispered, my head leaning close to his. I could smell the ozone of his armor and the faint, sharp scent of his sweat. "I can disrupt its logic-gate for three seconds. That’s our window."

"Three seconds is a lifetime, Caelia," Kaelen murmured. He looked down at me, his violet eyes dark in the shadows. There was no doubt in them. No hesitation. He trusted my "Prodigy" intuition more than he trusted his own sensors. "Do it."

I closed my eyes and reached out. 

I didn't use my hands. I used the resonance in my chest. I felt the Sentinel’s connection to the central hub—a thin, vibrating string of data. I didn't break it. I just... nudged it. I created a phantom ping thirty feet behind us.

The Sentinel’s head snapped back. It pivoted, its violet eye searching for the ghost I’d created.

"Go," I gasped, the effort sending a spike of heat through my core.

We blurred across the hallway. Kaelen caught me as we reached the heavy bulkhead of the Vault, his arm wrapping around my waist to steady me as the "Prodigy" crash hit. 

"You're red-lining," he said, his voice a rasp against my ear.

"I’m fine," I lied, my lungs burning. "Open the door, Commander. Let’s get what we came for."

# CHAPTER 16: THE PRIME CORE

The room was a cathedral of glass. In the center, suspended in a cylinder of liquid amber, was the Core. It pulsed with a rhythmic, golden light that made my own blood sing in recognition.

"It’s beautiful," Silas said, stepping into the room behind us. He had been quiet during the climb, his eyes scanning the monitors. 

Kaelen approached the console, his gloved fingers moving with lethal precision. "Beauty is a marketing term for high-yield weapons, Silas. Caelia, I need the interface. Connect."

I stepped up to the terminal. The interface ports looked like open wounds in the chrome. I took the cable—the physical tether that would link my brain to the Imperial network—and hesitated.

"If I do this," I said, looking at Kaelen, "there’s no going back. The Empress will see my signature. She’ll know I’m the one who stole the keys to her kingdom."

Kaelen stepped closer, his hand coming up to cup the side of my face. His thumb brushed over the scar near my eye, a touch so unexpectedly tender it made my breathing hitch. "You aren't a battery anymore, Caelia. You’re the pilot. And I’m your shield. No one gets to you while I’m standing."

I nodded, the "Gifted Kid" in me surrendering to the "Soldier" he’d trained. 

I pushed the cable into the port at the base of my skull.

The world vanished. I wasn't in a room anymore; I was in the stream. Millions of data points screamed past me. I felt the Prime Core—the massive, ancient consciousness of a sleeping god—and I reached for it.

*Accessing...*
*Decrypting...*
*Authorized.*

"I have it," I whispered, the gold light from the cylinder reflecting in my eyes until they glowed like dying stars. "Kaelen, I have the Core."

The girl who entered the Iron Lung was dead. And the machine that replaced her was just getting started.
